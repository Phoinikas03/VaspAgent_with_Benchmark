<p><a class="mw-selflink selflink">BANDGAP</a> = COMPACT | WEIGHT | KPOINT<br/> Default: <b>BANDGAP</b> = COMPACT 
</p>
<p>Description: <a class="mw-selflink selflink">BANDGAP</a> determines the verbosity for reporting the bandgap to the OUTCAR file.
Only the first character is significant.
</p>
<p>The bandgap of the system separates the occupied valence bands from the unoccupied conduction bands.
Of particular interest are the fundamental bandgap between valence band maximum (VBM) and conduction band minimum (CBM) and the direct bandgap at a single <b>k</b> point.
</p>
<p><a class="mw-selflink selflink">BANDGAP</a> controls how VASP reports the bandgap information.
The following options exist:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-selflink selflink">BANDGAP</a>=COMPACT</li></ul>
<ul><li><a class="mw-selflink selflink">BANDGAP</a>=WEIGHT</li></ul>
<ul><li><a class="mw-selflink selflink">BANDGAP</a>=KPOINT</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Difference_between_WEIGHT_and_KPOINT">Difference between WEIGHT and KPOINT</span></h2>
<p>The figure illustrates different possible properties of electronic band structures.
For semiconductors, the bandgap is well defined so that the choice of <a class="mw-selflink selflink">BANDGAP</a> only affects whether the output is compact or verbose.
For metals, there is no straightforward definition of a bandgap so the default of <a class="mw-selflink selflink">BANDGAP</a>=COMPACT is sufficient.
A semimetal shares properties with metals (Fermi energy crosses bands) and with semiconductors (visible bandgap in the band structure).
</p>
<p><a class="mw-selflink selflink">BANDGAP</a>=WEIGHT interprets systems like a metal; valence (conduction) states are more (less) than half-filled.
In this case, the bandgap of a semimetal converges to zero with an increasing number of <b>k</b> points similar to a metal.
For N number of electrons, the number of valence states is not in general equal to N for all <b>k</b> points.
Hence, the direct bandgap is not necessarily between the N-th and the (N + 1)-th state.
</p>
<p><a class="mw-selflink selflink">BANDGAP</a>=KPOINT considers systems like a semiconductor where every <b>k</b> point may be treated individually.
The number of valence bands is equal to the number of electrons N for all <b>k</b> points and the direct bandgap is equal to the smallest difference between the N-th and (N + 1)-th state.
In a semimetal, this approach produces a negative bandgap because the Fermi energy crosses the bands.
For collinear calculations (<a href="/wiki/index.php/ISPIN" title="ISPIN">ISPIN</a>=2), this method will allow for a different number of up and down electrons at every <b>k</b> point.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_of_the_verbose_output">Example of the verbose output</span></h2>
<p>The column <i>spin independent</i> is always present and reports the bandgap ignoring the spin of the electron.
The other two columns are only visible for <a href="/wiki/index.php/ISPIN" title="ISPIN">ISPIN</a>=2 and describe the bandgap for a given spin of the electron.
The first block of rows show the band edges of the fundamental bandgap and their corresponding <b>k</b>-point coordinates.
The second block reports analogous values for the direct gap.
In each case the energy difference of the band edges is computed and printed as fundamental and direct gap, respectively.
</p>
