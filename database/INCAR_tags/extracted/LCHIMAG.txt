<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_tags">General tags</span></h3>
<p>The first block of tags in the <a href="/wiki/index.php/INCAR" title="INCAR">INCAR</a> above expresses the fact that the calculations of chemical shifts by means of linear response often require a high accuracy:

<li>Large <a href="/wiki/index.php/ENCUT" title="ENCUT">ENCUT</a>).</li></ul>
<p>The chemical shifts are calculated from the orbital magnetic response under the assumption that the system is an insulator. Smearing schemes intended for metals can generate nonsense.
</p>
<ul><li>Use <a href="/wiki/index.php/ISMEAR" title="ISMEAR">ISMEAR</a>=0 and make <a href="/wiki/index.php/SIGMA" title="SIGMA">SIGMA</a> so small that states have no fractional occupancies.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NMR_specific_tags">NMR specific tags</span></h3>
<p>The second block of tags switches on the calculation of the chemical shifts (<a class="mw-selflink selflink">LCHIMAG</a>=.TRUE.), and controls several aspects of the finite difference <i>k</i>-space derivatives (Eqs. 38, 40, and 47 in the work of Yates <i>et al.</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yates:prb:2007_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-yates:prb:2007-2">[2]</a></sup>):
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/index.php/DQ" title="DQ">DQ</a> is the step size for the finite difference k-space derivative.
<ul><li>Typical values are in the range [0.001 - 0.003]. The default (<a href="/wiki/index.php/DQ" title="DQ">DQ</a>=0.001) is often sufficient.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/index.php/ICHIBARE" title="ICHIBARE">ICHIBARE</a> determines the order of the finite difference stencil used to calculate the magnetic susceptibility (second order derivative in Eq. 47 of Yates <i>et al.</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yates:prb:2007_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-yates:prb:2007-2">[2]</a></sup>). Note that magnetic susceptibility is a bulk property, while chemical shift is for each nucleus <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yates:prb:2007_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-yates:prb:2007-2">[2]</a></sup>.
<ul><li>A higher <a href="/wiki/index.php/ICHIBARE" title="ICHIBARE">ICHIBARE</a> results in a substantial increase of the computational load.</li></ul></li>
<li>For <a href="/wiki/index.php/NLSPLINE" title="NLSPLINE">NLSPLINE</a>=.TRUE., the PAW projectors in reciprocal space (<a href="/wiki/index.php/LREAL" title="LREAL">LREAL</a>=.FALSE.) are set up using a spline interpolation so that they are <i>k</i>-differentiable.
<ul><li>This improves the susceptibility contribution to the shifts (via the aforementioned Eq. 47). Other contributions to the shifts (Eqs. 38 and 40) are only slightly affected.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/index.php/LNMR_SYM_RED" title="LNMR SYM RED">LNMR_SYM_RED</a>=.TRUE. is recommended. The star on which the <i>k</i>-space derivative is calculated is oriented along the cartesian directions in <i>k</i>-space. If the symmetry operations in <i>k</i>-space do not map this star onto itself, erroneous results can be obtained. <a href="/wiki/index.php/LNMR_SYM_RED" title="LNMR SYM RED">LNMR_SYM_RED</a>=.TRUE. checks for such operations, discarding them, resulting in a larger IBZ.</li></ul>
<p>There are also some additional tags relevant to <a class="mw-selflink selflink">LCHIMAG</a> that are not used in the example <a href="/wiki/index.php/INCAR" title="INCAR">INCAR</a> that should be considered in special cases:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/index.php/LBONE" title="LBONE">LBONE</a> adds the small B-component to the chemical shift tensor <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewijs:laskowski:jcp:2017_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dewijs:laskowski:jcp:2017-3">[3]</a></sup>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/index.php/LVGVCALC" title="LVGVCALC">LVGVCALC</a> uses the vGv expression when calculating the orbital magnetic susceptibility <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avezac:prb:2007_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-avezac:prb:2007-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewijs:havenith:jcp:2021_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-dewijs:havenith:jcp:2021-5">[5]</a></sup>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/index.php/LVGVAPPL" title="LVGVAPPL">LVGVAPPL</a> uses vGv orbital magnetic susceptibility when calculating the chemical shift anisotropy (CSA) tensor.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/index.php/LLRAUG" title="LLRAUG">LLRAUG</a> calculates the two-center contributions to the chemical shielding tensor .</li></ul>
<p>The chemical shift is calculated via the induced current (cf. <a class="new" href="/wiki/index.php?title=LWRTCUR&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="LWRTCUR (page does not exist)">LWRTCUR</a>) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pickard:prb:2001_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-pickard:prb:2001-1">[1]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yates:prb:2007_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-yates:prb:2007-2">[2]</a></sup>.
It has contributions from the plane wave grid and one-center contributions (the induced field
at the center of a PAW sphere due to the augmentation current inside that sphere). Two-center contributions (induced fields due to augmentation currents in
other PAW spheres) are standard neglected. These contributions can be switched on using <a href="/wiki/index.php/LLRAUG" title="LLRAUG">LLRAUG</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Output">Output</span></h2>
<p>The isotropic chemical shieldings are printed towards the end of the <a href="/wiki/index.php/OUTCAR" title="OUTCAR">OUTCAR</a> file, after the self-consistent calculation has finished. The chemical shift tensors both before and after space group symmetrization. These are the absolute tensors for the infinite lattice, excluding core contributions. They can be searched for under the <code>UNSYMMETRIZED TENSORS</code> and <code>SYMMETRIZED TENSORS</code> after <code>Absolute Chemical Shift tensors</code>. Additionally, the magnetic susceptibility is printed shortly after and found under <code>ORBITAL MAGNETIC SUSCEPTIBILITY</code>.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advice_and_recommendations">Advice and recommendations</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Input_parameters">Input parameters</span></h3>
<ul><li>A larger <a href="/wiki/index.php/ENCUT" title="ENCUT">ENCUT</a> value than usual, generally much higher than the value given by ENMAX in the <a href="/wiki/index.php/POTCAR" title="POTCAR">POTCAR</a> file, e.g. 800 eV for C.</li>
<li>A small <a href="/wiki/index.php/EDIFF" title="EDIFF">EDIFF</a> is typically required to provide converged chemical shifts, e.g. <code>1E-8</code> eV.</li>
<li>Tighter precision, e.g. <a href="/wiki/index.php/PREC" title="PREC">PREC</a> = Accurate.</li></ul>
<p>Two additional terms may make a difference depending on your system, which should be tested with and without to determine their importance: 
</p>
<ul><li>Non-spherical contributions to the gradient of the density inside PAW spheres, i.e. <a href="/wiki/index.php/LASPH" title="LASPH">LASPH</a> = .TRUE.</li>
<li>Occasionally, e.g. for systems containing H or first-row elements, and short bonds, the two-center contributions are important. In this case, <a href="/wiki/index.php/LLRAUG" title="LLRAUG">LLRAUG</a> = .TRUE. should be used <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewijs:jcp:2013_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-dewijs:jcp:2013-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dewijs:jcp:2021_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-dewijs:jcp:2021-9">[9]</a></sup>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PAW_pseudopotentials">PAW pseudopotentials</span></h3>
<p>No special <a href="/wiki/index.php/POTCAR" title="POTCAR">POTCAR</a> files are necessary. The GIPAW is applied using the projectors functions and partial waves that are stored in the regular <a href="/wiki/index.php/POTCAR" title="POTCAR">POTCAR</a> files. A few remarks, however, on accuracy in relation to the different <a href="/wiki/index.php/POTCAR" title="POTCAR">POTCAR</a> flavours:
</p>
<ul><li>Results sensitively depend on the quality, <i>i.e.</i>, completeness of the partial wave/projector function set in the energy range needed for good chemical transferability. Result obtained with different <a href="/wiki/index.php/POTCAR" title="POTCAR">POTCAR</a> flavours can differ a few ppm for first and second row <i>sp</i>-bonded elements (except for H).</li>
<li>Use <a href="/wiki/index.php/POTCAR" title="POTCAR">POTCAR</a> files generated with a consistent exchange-correlation functional. The PAW reconstruction with AE partial waves is crucial as the field on the nucleus needs to be calculated. So avoid, if possible, overriding <tt>LEXCH</tt> from <a href="/wiki/index.php/POTCAR" title="POTCAR">POTCAR</a> with an explicit <a href="/wiki/index.php/GGA" title="GGA">GGA</a>-tag in the <a href="/wiki/index.php/INCAR" title="INCAR">INCAR</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insufficient_memory">Insufficient memory</span></h3>
<p>In the case of insufficient memory, there are a few options. Speed has been favored over saving memory. Since the linear response calculation is parallel over <i>k</i>-points, this can be used to economize on memory:
</p>
<ul><li>First, do a regular self-consistent calculation at high accuracy for the full <i>k</i>-point mesh. Save the <a href="/wiki/index.php/CHGCAR" title="CHGCAR">CHGCAR</a> file.</li>
<li>Second, do a chemical shift calculation for each <i>k</i>-point in the IBZ separately, starting from <a href="/wiki/index.php/CHGCAR" title="CHGCAR">CHGCAR</a>, <i>i.e.</i>, using <a href="/wiki/index.php/ICHARG" title="ICHARG">ICHARG</a>=11.</li>
<li>Finally, calculate the shifts as a <i>k</i>-point weighted average of the symmetrized shifts of the individual <i>k</i>-points.</li></ul>
